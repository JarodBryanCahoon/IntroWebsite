Fundamentals of Owning an Aquarium

  • Tank Supplies

    • Fish Tank - When buying a fishtank, there are several things that you should consider about your purchase. One of the first, and most fundamental things that you should consider about your tank is what type of material you would like the tank to be made out of. Tanks are typically made out of either acryllic or glass, both of which are very similar, but slightly different. Acryllic is typically tougher than glass, though it is often much softer, and will scratch more easily. Glass, on the other hand, is harder than glass, but is less tough, and runs a higher risk of being shattered during transportation. The next thing you should consider when buying a tank is the size of the tank. Tanks can range from 1 gallon, to 200 plus, and for good reason. Certain animals will often require more room than other animals in tanks, which should be considered. Colleges/apartments sometimes have regulations on the maximum size of fishtanks, so check those requirements before you buy a tank. Additionally, keep in mind that water is extremely heavy, and tanks larger than 15 gallons or so require specialized stands that can withstand the weight of the tank. A 30 gallon tank contains ~250 pounds of water, which can make some full tanks approach 300 pounds fully filled. Petsmart often has "dollar per gallon" deals, in which they sale tanks up to around 75 gallons for around a dollar a gallon. Second-hand tanks are often a good option, with both Ebay and craigslist commonly having listings for tanks.
    • Filter - Filters are needed to keep the water of a fishtank clean, and are imperative for the health of the animals in the tank. There are several different styles of fishtank filters, from sponge filters to traditional filters, to even bio-balls. Each of these function in a slightly different way, but ultimately serve the same purpose. Depending on what type of tank/size of tank that you have, and what resides in it, one filter may be preferable to another.
    • Vacuum Cleaner - Though the filter on the fishtank make keep the water itself clean enough for fish to swim in, they typically are not powerful enough to remove all plant detritus/excess food from falling to the bottom of the tank. If left uncleaned at the bottom of the tank, this food/detritus can decompose and cause an ammonia spike in your tank, which could potentially kill your fish if not quickly handled. Most of the vacuums for aquariums are simple hand pumps with a grate to prevent substrate from being sucked out, though nicer models, with actual motors can also be bought. Since fish tanks can already be pricey, I think that a hand pump is adequate for the most part.
    • Thermometer/Heater - Most fish/invertibrates that you will be storing in a fish tank will be extremely temperature sensitive, which makes it necessary to not only have a way to measure the temperature, but also a way to ensure that the water is warm. These items can be found cheaply on Amazon or in your local fish store, but I would specifically recommend a heater which automatically maintains a certain temperature.
    • Light - Just like humans, most fish can have a circadian rhythm, and be photosensitive. Typically, unless your tank is stored directly adjacent to a window, it can be difficult for an unlit tank to get adequate light. For this reason, it is recommended for most tanks that you buy a light to either attach to the hood, or place directly above the open tank. Most fish tank "beginner kits" come with a light. While it is important that you do provide a light for your tank, it is perhaps more important that the light does not stay on longer than ~6 hours a day. Prolonged light exposure in a tank can cause excess algae to bloom, which could damage the health of the tank. For this, I would recommend a programmable switch , which can be used to set the hours in which your light should turn or off. If you are interested in having a planted tank, then the lighting of the tank becomes even more important, and there are customizable lights which allow you to specify the wavelength of light you want, to maximize the growth of your plants.
    • Surface Agitation - The last major component of any good tank setup is something which "agitates" the water's surface, or causes it to move. In nature, most inhabitable bodies of water are constantly in motion, be it from whatever force. This motion causes the surface of the water to continuously coalesce and overlap with the air around it, doing two distinct things. First, this motion causes oxygen from the air to be introduced, and eventually dissolved, into the water. Second, this causes any biological buildup on the surface of the water to be continually mixed back into the water, and not allowed to settle on the top. Because a fish tank (hopefully) will not be subject to any storms or violent shaking, this means that it needs to be introduced into the system deliberately. The main way of doing this is through the use of an air stone, though if you purchased a sponge filter they commonly do this by default. Regardless of which option you pick, however, an air pump and tubing are required.

  • Cycling a Tank

    • Cycling a fishtank is the act of creating a healthy bacterial environment within the fishtank, so that ammonia waste produced by organisms within the tank is properly handled. This cycle of breaking down ammonia is known as the Nitrogen Cycle.
    • This cycle is essential to ensure that all of the critters living in your tank are as healthy as possible. Low ( > 0) ppm of ammonia and nitrites in the water can make your animals lethargic or sick, and high ppm of ammonia and aitrites in the water can kill your animals outright, depending on the hardiness of the species.
    • Only after a fishtank has been cycled should animals be placed in your tank. Though you have already completed a cycle of the tank at this point, you should continue to monitor the water quality of the tank multiple times a week to ensure that no ammonia/nitrite/nitrate spikes occur.

  • Cleaning a Tank

    • Cleaning a tank is essential to ensuring that the water your pets live in does not become contaminated by their own waste, or excess food.
    • Typically, a tank should be cleaned about once a week, or even more if you have an especially productive tank which produces a lot of waste.
    • Cleaning involves vacuuming as much of the detritus and extra food out of substrate of the tank as possible, and then replacing this water with water that has been treated to remove chlorine or any other additives which may make your animals sick.
    • The glass of the tank should also be cleaned regularly, not only for your viewing pleasure, but also to prevent algae from taking over your tank, and potentially poisoning all of your animals. Cleaning the glass can consist of simply wiping out the tank with the a clean washcloth or paper towel, or purchasing animals which can clean the algae off of the glass.
    • You should also regularly perform water changes in the tank. This can often be done at the same time as cleaning the gravel, as most gravel vacuums also suck up the water. Performing regular water changes is necessary to removing any built up chemicals in the water which are too heavy or difficult for the bacterial medium in your filter to remove.

  • Freshwater vs Saltwater Tanks

    • This is an extremely complicated topic that I have very limited knowledge about, so I will keep it simple
    • Saltwater tanks, and consequently saltwater animals, are much harder to keep healthy and maintain than freshwater tanks and freshwater fish.
    • Consequently, I would strongly recommend that you do not try a saltwater tank if you are a beginner aquarist, or if you have not sat down and made a plan for all of the items, or how you will afford them.

  • Tankmate Compatability

    • Just like humans, some fish/invertebrates do not get along with one another. In a fishtank where there are many potential predator/prey relationships at stake, this means that you could potentially lose a lot of money on animals that you bought.
    • Before purchasing your primary tank inhabitant, consider whether or not adding this inhabitant to the tank would prevent you from successfully fostering another inhabitant in the future. For instance, male Betta fish are very aggressive, and will attack a huge swath of other animals, so purchasing a male Betta instantly eliminates a lot of other potential tankmates as options.
    • Before purchasing any additional tank inhabitants, ensure that what you currently have in the tank will not attack what you are purchasing, or vice versa. In addition, consider that it can take up to several weeks for a new tank inhabitant to "settle in", meaning that it is possible for aggression to occur between species which otherwise would not be aggressive towards one another.

Personal Favorite Tank Inhabitants

  • African Dwarf Frog - genus Hymenochirus

    • These freshwater frogs are both super interesting to look at and super interesting to interact with. Because they are fully aquatic, only needing to come up and get air from time to time, they work well in freshwater tanks. They are also a nice breath of fresh air from the typical tank inhabitants, which are usually fish or invertebrates.

  • Harlequin Shrimp - Hymenocera picta

    • The harlequin shrimp is an awesome looking saltwater shrimp whose diet consists of strictly one animal : starfish. This makes the harlequin shrimp incredibly hard to house and maintain, since you also have to actively grow a food supply in another tank (starfish are insanely expensive to buy).

  • Pea Puffer - Carinotetraodon travancoricus

    • Having caught (and released) actual puffer fish before off of the coast of North Carolina, I think its really cool that there is a mini version of what is essentially the same animal. I think the dorky little things are so adorable.

  • Bamboo Shrimp - Atyopsis

    • These dudes are cool, largely because they actually eat by waving their fans through the water and catching debris and small bacteria. They are also very amicable and fit in well with most community shrimp tanks.
